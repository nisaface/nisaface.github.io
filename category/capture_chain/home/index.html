SPLASH
THE GLOBAL CAPTURE CHAIN

In this chapter, I trace how the capture chains of the Police Action in Korea became important sites where the assumptions and ambiguities of Cold War geopolitics played out on captives in distinct ways. The chapter situates capture on the peninsula within longer histories of colonial and civil-war policing, and highlights how logistical systems, proxy forces, and classificatory regimes often generated disorder and displaced violence onto both civilians and prisoners.

The chapter then turns to EPW camps as laboratories for technoscientific experimentation, where Cold War behavioral scientists sought to transform illegibility and erratic battlefield decisions into calculable and anticipatory knowledge. Calculability emerged as a racialized response to uncertainty, transforming the supposed pathologies of Asian life into abstract data that could be governed and circulated—even as these very practices reproduced racial hierarchies and obscured the violence on which they relied.

Through interactive maps, archival images, drawings, and data visualizations, this page does not seek to reproduce or summarize the chapter so much as to open new pathways through its archives. Rather than functioning as a duplicate of the book’s argument, that is, these materials invite readers to move across scales in ways that exceed the linear form of print or through interactive media which are difficult to create in a printed book.

Japan's Colonial Prisons in 1910

Antecedents to the US Carceral Infrastructure in Korea

This map outlines the carceral geography of Japanese colonial Korea before 1921, where detention facilities and police networks became central instruments of imperial rule. Across the thirty-five years of often-brutal occupation, colonial officials portrayed Koreans as "uncivilized," unclean, and lacking a modern work ethic, and used these racialized claims to justify lower wages, restricted mobility, and pervasive surveillance.1 As the colonial state expanded, carceral infrastructures did as well, with the Japanese building 12 more facilities in 1921. 2

Colonial Prisons into the 1930s

The Contradictions of Colonial Modernity

The Japanese eventually moved from the largely punitive use of detention towards a regime that deployed the language of modern penology. As part of this shift, a two-tiered detainment system emerged, with one tier framed around a dispassionate, rational system of corrections and the other, primarily for political prisoners, that marshaled cruelty and torture in the pursuit of state order. As the colonial penal apparatus developed, these two carceral paths fused into a single spatial expression of the contradictions of colonial modernity.

Colonial Prisons into the 1930s

Colonial Modernity in Policy

Policies like the 1941 Preventive Detention Order authorized the detention of political actors before the commission of any crime or rebellious act, institutionalizing the idea that certain people were inherently dangerous and therefore outside the framework of rights. Those deemed irredeemable were subjected to violence and exclusion, while others who could be “reintegrated with the spirit of the Japanese nation” were disciplined through the techniques of modern penology.

The colonial prison thus functioned simultaneously as a laboratory of modern discipline and an instrument of imperial domination. Under the rhetoric of moral reform, it sorted and policed anti-imperial dissent, determining who could be incorporated into the settler-colonial order and who would be excluded through coercion and violence.3

3 Chulwoo Lee, “Modernity, Legality, and Power in Korea Under Japanese Rule,” in Colonial Modernity in Korea, ed. Michael Edson Robinson and Gi-Wook Shin (Harvard University Asia Center, 2001), 42.

US Military Government: 1945-1948

Colonial modernity and the US military government

With the surrender of Japan in August 1945, the United States arrived on the peninsula, and shortly thereafter established the United States Army Military Government in Korea (USAMGIK). The military government quickly implemented a series of policy decisions—about the army, police, bureaucracy, and judiciary—that would set the terms for the subsequent three years of occupation as well as impact the ongoing internal conflicts and the Korean War that followed.

US Military Government: 1945-1948

Colonial Afterlives

In the immediate aftermath of liberation, the U.S. military government in Korea inherited a colonial carceral system that remained largely intact from the Japanese occupation, mapped above.4 Americans quickly came to rely on and reinforce an updated form of the Korean National Police (KNP) structure that existed during the Japanese colonial period, many of whom were affiliated with or had collaborated with the repressive colonial regime. Supported financially and administratively by inexperienced American military advisors and counter-intelligence services, the KNP made extensive use of preemptive and administrative detention, police violence, and other extreme measures of social control.

1950–1953

EPW Facilities During the Police Action

Traditional narratives about the Police Action in Korea locate its beginning when the North Koreans launched an attack across the 38th Parallel on June 25th, 1950. Two weeks later, the United Nations Command chose a location for the first Enemy Prisoner of War (EPW) site in Pusan, and two just weeks later, the recently-completed enclosure was already full, necessitating a move to a new location there. In the chaos of the war’s first days, U.N. guards frequently moved scores of detainees within and between enclosures, camps, and newly-constructed replacement camps — often outrunning the nascent system meant to keep track of them. The resulting prison infrastructure is mapped here, showing the system as of 1953.6

1950–1953

Pusan's Carceral Logistics

Captives during the conflict were constantly in motion, traversing battlefields and transport corridors, often passing through the conflict’s logistical heart in the port of Pusan. As historian Patrick Chung observes, wartime demands propelled US Army engineers to radically refashion the city’s infrastructure by expanding wharves and piers, repairing and constructing roads and railways, installing telecommunications, and extending fuel supply lines.6 These projects, many rooted in Japanese colonial extractive regimes, were retooled to meet global US military standards. In doing so, they embedded Pusan in transnational supply networks that enabled not only the circulation of materiel but also facilitated the movement of captives through the war’s major capture chains.

6 Patrick Chung, “From Supply Lines to Supply Chains: Busan, the Korean War, and the Rise of Global Logistics,” in Korea and the World: New Frontiers in Korean Studies, ed. Gregg A. Brazinsky (Rowman & Littlefield, 2019), 15.

1950–1953

Urban War Prison Mobilities

The main UN military interrogation center was in the city, so captives from across the peninsula were constantly funneling through its streets on their way to EPW camps. By December, the US employed approximately 900 PWs working in three shifts, using sixty trucks daily to move captives between the port and enclosures. The 70th Transportation Truck Battalion alone used forty-five tractor-trailers per day for this purpose.7

1950–1953

Prisoners beyond the wire

The logistics of caring for large captive populations while conducting war had dramatic effects on local markets for food, fuel, and land. Further, local currency fluctuation, contract breaches, timber and energy costs, local and worldwide food shortages, inadequate or improper storage, Weevil infestation, fire, theft, and container damages all made wartime carceral care a regional challenge.8

October 1953

Shifting Populations

When Chinese forces entered the peninsula in late 1950, UN troops evacuated Wonsan, Pyongyang, and Inchon, closing large EPW camps and transferring prisoners to facilities near Pusan. As the main port for UN personnel and supplies, the city quickly became overcrowded, and the influx of military units, prisoners, refugees, and war materiel forced the Provost Marshal to focus Military Police resources on port clearance and traffic control. In this situation, EPWs were simultaneously obstacles to movement and essential sources of labor sustaining this aspect of the war. Ultimately, this cacophonous circulation created a security risk, and UN Forces decided that the enemy prisoner installations near Pusan needed to be relocated.

The southern island of Koje-do was chosen as the new site for the installations. This map shows a snapshot of war prison populations in 1953, well after this transfer and the protracted period of negotiations that led to the armistice agreement.

Koje-do

The Island Camp

The geography of Koje-do posed immense challenges for camp construction. General Matthew Ridgway, commander of the Eighth Army and later all UN forces in Korea, recalled in his memoir that the island was “hardly the ground a sane man would have chosen to erect camp sites. It was rocky and mountainous, with almost no flat ground for construction and proper dispersal of compounds.”9 Before the war, the island’s few flat areas had been intensively farmed, and by the time prisoners arrived, tens of thousands of refugees had also settled there, further constricting available land for enclosures.

9 Gen Matthew Ridgway, The Korean War: How We Met the Challenge (Doubleday, 1967, 1967), 206.

Koje-do, 1952

Rebellion Behind the Wire

On Koje-do, chronic shortages of guards, administrative personnel, and reliable supply chains destabilized camp governance at Koje Do, contributing to disorder, violence, and recurring crises of control. In May of 1952 organized Communist detainees took Brigadier General Francis Dodd hostage in an attempt to bargain for, among other things, control of their relocation and repatriation and better camp conditions. This and other events on Koje-do, as well as others across the conlfict's duration, would underpin calls for new prisoner handling directives. This included on UN study that boldly argued that “the Geneva Conventions of 1949 should be re-examined and revised to allow the detaining power or powers to properly cope with the situation created by armed conflict with fanatical PWs whose countries do not subscribe to the Geneva Conventions.”10

Koje-do

The Island Camp

The construction of the Koje-do camps reveals how wartime detention depended on coercive land seizure, improvised infrastructure, and the extensive mobilization of captive labor under conditions of scarcity and crisis. The military requisitioned land without compensation and built the camps rapidly and unevenly, relying heavily on prisoners themselves to clear terrain, construct enclosures, and make the sites minimally habitable amid shortages of materials, equipment, and skilled personnel. Environmental conditions and logistical failures further destabilized the camps, producing severe sanitation and health crises. Rather than purpose-built or orderly spaces, the Koje-do camps emerged as fragile, persistently unfinished installations, shaped as much by circulation and improvisation as by formal planning. 8086 Army Unit, Logistical Support to Prisoners of War July 1951-July 1953, 14.

During the Cold War, major security narratives may well have been constructed around containing (or rolling back) a well-defined ideological and territorial foe. The political decision had seemingly been made. But rather than a space for the rational use of counter-force, the complexities of life along the capture chain often blurred the distinction between friend and enemy, civilian and combatant, yielding a confounding illegibility that often amplified the scale of war's barbarity.

In this chapter, I trace how the capture chains of the Korean War became central sites where these assumptions and ambiguities of Cold War geopolitics took concrete form. I show how uncertainty about allegiance, intention, and colonial afterlives did not merely complicate the geographies of carceral care but actively produced new sites and forms of violence.
